## Week 25: Pre-Competition Planning

**Module:** Match-day routines | **Core Skill:** Structured match preparation | **Age Bands:** All | **Duration:** 20 min | **Format:** Match-day plan construction + simulation

**Evidence Basis:** Moran (1996): structured pre-competition preparation reduces cognitive anxiety and enhances perceived readiness. Gould et al. (2002) Olympic athlete study — match-day routines are universal among peak performers.

### Part 1 — Opening (3 min)

> "Match day. What's your current morning routine? From waking up to kick-off?"

(Most have no structured routine — or a chaotic one.)

> "Phase 3 is where we take everything you've learned and apply it under actual competitive pressure. It starts with owning your match day."

### Part 2 — Match-Day Architecture (7 min)

Build a match-day plan with five phases:

1. Wake (–3 hours): Sleep hygiene, meal, hydration, limited social media
2. Arrival (–90 min): Music (arousal-regulation playlist), positive self-talk check
3. Physical warm-up (–45 min): Standard + mental warm-up added
4. Pre-game routine (–10 min): Full 5-step routine (Week 7)
5. Kick-off: Intent word locked in

> "This isn't about luck. It's about arriving at kick-off in the same psychological state every time."

### Part 3 — Full Routine Run-Through (8 min)

Simulate the final 10 minutes before a game:

- Complete silence (coach enforces)
- Each player performs their full routine
- Coach observes: who is composed? Who is distracted? Who reverts to phone use?
- Feedback after: "What did it feel like to actually protect that mental space?"

### Part 4 — Match-Day Plan Card (2 min)

```
MY MATCH-DAY PLAN

Wake-up time: _____ | I eat: _____ at _____
Travel: I listen to ____________
Arrival: I do ____________
Pre-game (–10 min): my 5-step routine
Kick-off word: ____________
```

### Age Adaptations

- **Juniors (7–9):** Parent-facing version. Provide parents with a "match morning" guide.
- **Seniors (13–14):** Include game analysis prep: "What are my 3 tactical/mental intentions for today's game?"

## Week 26: Half-Time Resets

**Module:** In-game adjustment | **Core Skill:** Mid-game mental reset | **Age Bands:** All | **Duration:** 20 min | **Format:** Half-time simulation + protocol

**Evidence Basis:** McCarthy (2011): athletes who use structured half-time recovery periods show significantly better second-half performance. Jones (1995): the ability to re-evaluate and reframe mid-competition is a hallmark of resilient performance.

### Part 1 — Opening (3 min)

> "You're losing 2-0 at half-time. Your team is flat. You have 10 minutes. What do you do with them?"

> "Most teams spend those 10 minutes on tactics. The best teams combine tactics with psychology. You're learning the psychology part."

### Part 2 — The Half-Time Protocol (5 min)

Five-minute half-time mental reset sequence:

1. Physiological recovery (1 min): breathe, hydrate, slow heart rate
2. Honest appraisal (1 min): "What's working? What isn't? One thing each."
3. Tactical clarity (2 min): coach input — specific and concise
4. Mental reset (1 min): cue word, one confidence anchor, one process intent
5. Re-entry ritual (10 sec): team gesture + individual activation phrase

### Part 3 — Half-Time Protocol Drill (10 min)

- Run a full small-sided game (8 min — 4 min each half)
- At half-time (4 min): execute the full 5-step protocol
- Coach facilitates — does NOT fill the time with tactics only
- Observe: does the team's energy change in the second half?

### Part 4 — Takeaway (2 min)

```
MY HALF-TIME MENTAL CHECKLIST

1. Breathe + hydrate: ☐
2. One thing working: ____________
3. One thing to change: ____________
4. My cue word for second half: ____________
5. Team intent: ____________
```

### Age Adaptations

- **Juniors (7–9):** Simplify to three steps: rest, breathe, one word. Parent/coach models it.
- **Seniors (13–14):** Add leadership element — "Who leads the psychological reset in this group, and how?"

## Week 27: Post-Game Processing

**Module:** Healthy reflection | **Core Skill:** Performance review | **Age Bands:** All | **Duration:** 20 min | **Format:** Debrief protocol

**Evidence Basis:** Gibbs (1988) reflective cycle adapted for sport: description → feelings → evaluation → analysis → conclusion → action plan. Gould et al. (2002): athletes who review performance systematically develop faster than those who rely on intuition.

### Part 1 — Opening (2 min)

> "What do most players do in the car home after a bad game? Replay it over and over. What about after a good game? Forget it by Monday."

> "Both are opportunities wasted. Today we learn to process both."

### Part 2 — The Post-Game Debrief (6 min)

Four questions — always asked in this order:

1. Factual: What happened? (No evaluation — just facts)
2. Emotional: How did I feel? (Name it specifically)
3. Performance: What went well? What didn't? (Balanced view)
4. Forward: One thing I'll do differently. One thing I'll keep.

> "Avoid the trap of emotional contamination — letting the result determine your evaluation. A good performance in a lost game is still a good performance."

### Part 3 — Post-Game Debrief Practice (10 min)

Pairs conduct a structured post-game debrief on today's session using the 4-question framework.

- Partner listens without interrupting for the first 3 questions
- Partner only speaks for question 4 — adding one observation

### Part 4 — Performance Review Template (2 min)

```
POST-GAME PERFORMANCE REVIEW

What happened (facts): ____________
How I felt: ____________
Performance highlight: ____________
What to adjust: ____________
One thing I'll do differently: ____________
```

### Age Adaptations

- **Juniors (7–9):** Three questions only. Use "good, hard, next" — one thing that was good, one that was hard, one I'll try next time.
- **Seniors (13–14):** Add quantitative self-rating (1–10 on focus, composure, effort, tactical execution) and track over a season.

## Week 28: Identity and Athlete Mindset

**Module:** Self-concept | **Core Skill:** Athletic identity | **Age Bands:** All | **Duration:** 20 min | **Format:** Identity exploration + drill

**Evidence Basis:** Brewer et al. (1993): Athletic Identity Measurement Scale (AIMS) — strong athletic identity increases motivation but can also increase vulnerability to injury and de-selection. Waterman (1982): identity development is a core developmental task for adolescents and directly impacts self-regulation.

### Part 1 — Opening (3 min)

> "Who are you as a player? Not what position you play. Not your stats. WHO are you?"

> "Today's session is about the most powerful performance variable there is: your sense of self."

### Part 2 — Identity Exploration (7 min)

Four identity dimensions:

1. Technical self: "What are my actual skills? What am I known for?"
2. Psychological self: "How do I approach challenges? What's my temperament?"
3. Social self: "What do I contribute to the team? Who am I in this group?"
4. Aspirational self: "Who am I becoming? What's the player I'm building?"

> "A strong identity isn't about being the best. It's about knowing who you are in a way that is authentic and resilient."

### Part 3 — Identity in Pressure (8 min)

Small-sided game with a twist: deliberately introduce pressure moments (unfair decision, aggressive opponent, scoreboard visible).

- Coach observes: does the player's identity hold under pressure?
- Does self-doubt overtake their sense of who they are?
- Debrief: "Who stayed themselves today?"

### Part 4 — Identity Card (2 min)

```
MY IDENTITY CARD

I am a player who: ____________
My mental signature: ____________
My contribution to the team: ____________
I am becoming: ____________
```

### Age Adaptations

- **Juniors (7–9):** "What kind of footballer do you want to be?" Draw or describe.
- **Seniors (13–14):** Discuss the risks of over-identification with sport identity. "What would you have if football wasn't there?" Encourage multi-dimensionality.

### Safeguarding Note

Fragile or entirely football-dependent identity is a risk factor for mental health difficulties on injury or de-selection. Flag to pastoral lead if a player cannot identify a meaningful non-football self.

## Week 29: Performing Under Scrutiny

**Module:** Social pressure | **Core Skill:** Maintaining performance when observed | **Age Bands:** All | **Duration:** 20 min | **Format:** Observed pressure practice

**Evidence Basis:** Zajonc (1965): social facilitation — presence of others enhances performance of well-learned skills but impairs performance of novel/developing skills. Beilock & Carr (2001): scrutiny causes explicit monitoring of automatic skills (choking).

### Part 1 — Opening (2 min)

> "Do you perform differently when someone important is watching? A scout. Your parents. A coach from another team?"

> "Of course. That's human. Today we train to close the gap between your training performance and your observed performance."

### Part 2 — Why We Choke Under Scrutiny (5 min)

> "Here's what happens: someone important watches, your brain switches from automatic to manual. You start 'trying' instead of 'trusting'."

Two strategies:

1. Normalise the observer: "They're just watching a training session. The session is the point, not them."
2. Lock into process: "When observed, go to your cue word and your process intent — not your output."

### Part 3 — Observed Pressure Practice (11 min)

- Set up: normal drill
- Coach announces: "I'm going to watch specifically one player for the next 3 minutes. I won't tell you who."
- Everyone performs as if being watched (they are)
- Switch to: "Two guests are watching this next game. They're evaluating mental fitness, not football skill."
- Debrief: "What changed in your body? What did you do to stay focused?"

### Part 4 — Takeaway (2 min)

```
PERFORMING UNDER SCRUTINY

What happens in my body when I'm watched: ____________
My strategy for observers: ____________
My process cue word under scrutiny: ____________
```

### Age Adaptations

- **Juniors (7–9):** Keep it playful — "pretend everyone thinks you're brilliant." Reduce threat.
- **Seniors (13–14):** Trial runs. Bring in one outside observer (parent, older player) explicitly. Debrief directly.

## Week 30: Slumps and Form Dips

**Module:** Advanced resilience | **Core Skill:** Navigating extended poor form | **Age Bands:** All | **Duration:** 20 min | **Format:** Case study + toolkit building

**Evidence Basis:** Gould et al. (1993): athletes identify loss of form as among the top three psychological challenges. Orlick (1990): distinguishing between slumps (temporary performance dips) and plateaus (developmental stalls) requires different responses.

### Part 1 — Opening (3 min)

> "A slump is different from a bad game. It's a period — maybe two weeks, maybe six — where nothing clicks. Today we talk about what to do when that happens to you."

### Part 2 — Understanding Slumps (6 min)

Four causes of slumps:

1. Technical: Over-thinking a skill that used to be automatic
2. Physical: Fatigue, illness, growth spurts affecting coordination
3. Psychological: Loss of confidence creating a feedback loop
4. Environmental: Team changes, family stress, school pressure

Slump response toolkit:

- Go back to basics (simplest version of the skill)
- Rebuild confidence bank (only mastery drills, no competition)
- Narrow focus (one process goal per session)
- Separate results from performance quality
- Seek support (coach, trusted adult)

### Part 3 — Slump Response Simulation (8 min)

- Deliberately complex drill designed to fail (too many touches required, unfamiliar conditions)
- Players use their slump toolkit: simplify, focus, deposit
- Debrief: "What would you do differently if this was week 3 of struggling?"

### Part 4 — Case Study Analysis (3 min)

> "Name a professional player who came back from a visible slump. What did they do? What can you learn from it?"

```
MY SLUMP RESPONSE TOOLKIT

If I'm in a slump, first I'll: ____________
I'll track my recovery by: ____________
The person I'll talk to: ____________
My anchor process goal: ____________
```

### Age Adaptations

- **Juniors (7–9):** Focus on "bad patches" not "slumps" — language adjustment. Normalise. Use a story (fictional player who had a hard patch and came back).
- **Seniors (13–14):** Deep dive into the confidence-performance spiral. Discuss how elite athletes maintain motivation during slumps.

## Week 31: Leadership Skills

**Module:** Team psychology | **Core Skill:** Leadership and influence | **Age Bands:** All | **Duration:** 20 min | **Format:** Captain for a day + leadership journal

**Evidence Basis:** Cotterill & Fransen (2016): distributed leadership (multiple leaders in different roles) is more effective in sport than single-leader models. Gould et al. (2013): psychological skills training produces better leaders across all team members.

### Part 1 — Opening (2 min)

> "Leadership isn't just for captains. Every player leads something. Today everyone leads."

### Part 2 — Three Leadership Types (5 min)

1. Formal leadership: Captain, set-piece taker, organiser
2. Vocal leadership: Encourager, energiser, communicator
3. Quiet leadership: Role model, consistent performer, first to work hard

> "You don't have to be loud to lead. But you do have to be intentional."

Four leadership actions any player can take:

- Give one genuine specific compliment to a teammate today
- Call one teammate's name to encourage them during a difficult moment
- Ask "what do we need right now?" when energy drops
- Model the mental skill you've been developing

### Part 3 — Captain for the Day Drill (11 min)

- Each player takes a 3-minute stint as "mental fitness captain" during a drill
- Their role: encourage mental performance (not just technical) — cue words, resets, composure
- Not tactical direction — psychological support only
- Group feedback after each captain stint: "What did they do well? What would help?"

### Part 4 — Leadership Journal (2 min)

```
MY LEADERSHIP JOURNAL

My leadership type: ____________
One leadership action I'll take this week: ____________
Who in this team needs more encouragement? ____________
What I noticed about leading vs. following: ____________
```

### Age Adaptations

- **Juniors (7–9):** "Helper of the day" role — a player whose job is to encourage one teammate.
- **Seniors (13–14):** Discuss the research on peer leadership. Set a 4-week leadership challenge: each player takes one formal leadership action per session.

## Week 32: Reading the Game Mentally

**Module:** Tactical awareness | **Core Skill:** Cognitive performance in sport | **Age Bands:** All | **Duration:** 20 min | **Format:** Scenario-based drill

**Evidence Basis:** Williams & Reilly (2000): expert footballers process information differently — they make fewer fixations but extract more information. Mann et al. (2007): anticipation skill is trainable through systematic scenario exposure.

### Part 1 — Opening (3 min)

> "Football is played in your body AND in your head. The best players read the game two seconds ahead of everyone else. That's not talent — it's trained attention."

### Part 2 — Cognitive Game Reading (5 min)

Three mental habits that improve game reading:

1. Scanning before receiving: look before the ball arrives — every player, every time
2. Anticipatory self-talk: "If X happens, I do Y" — pre-load decisions
3. Mental pressure map: "Where is space? Who is pressed? Who is free?"

Introduce "If-Then" planning:

> "Before every session, programme two tactical triggers: 'If I'm pressed on the right, I'll switch left.' 'If we lose the ball, I'll recover to this position.'"

### Part 3 — Scenario-Based Drill (10 min)

- Freeze-frame drill: coach pauses the game every 60 seconds
- Players must answer (without looking): "Where are all teammates? Where is space? What's the next best action?"
- Resume — see if anticipation matches reality
- Debrief: "Who is reading the game? What's their mental habit?"

### Part 4 — Film Review Questions (2 min)

```
FILM REVIEW QUESTIONS (off-pitch)

Watch 5 minutes of a professional game this week and answer:

1. How many times does the player scan before receiving?
2. What decision are they making before the ball arrives?
3. What's one habit I want to copy?
```

### Age Adaptations

- **Juniors (7–9):** Simplify to "look before you touch." Reward scanning behaviour explicitly during drills.
- **Seniors (13–14):** Introduce expert-novice perception research. Use video (coach records session) for individual feedback on scanning patterns.

## Week 33: Injury and Mental Fitness

**Module:** Adversity | **Core Skill:** Psychological response to injury | **Age Bands:** All | **Duration:** 20 min | **Format:** Education + mindset session

**Evidence Basis:** Wiese-Bjornstal et al. (1998): psychological response to sport injury follows a predictable pattern — initial distress, adaptation, and re-entry. Clement & Shannon (2011): athletes with psychological skills training return from injury faster and with stronger mental fitness.

### Part 1 — Opening (3 min)

> "Injury is part of sport. It will happen to most of you. Today we talk about the mental side of injury — because nobody does."

### Part 2 — The Injury Mental Journey (7 min)

Three phases of injury response:

1. Shock and loss: "I can't play. Everything is on hold." Normal response — allow it.
2. Adjustment: "How do I use this time? What can I still train?" Shift from loss to adaptation.
3. Return: "I'm not starting from zero. My mental fitness training still counts."

Mental fitness during injury:

- Continue imagery and visualisation (shown to maintain neural pathways)
- Set recovery process goals
- Use the time to develop off-pitch skills (leadership, film review, self-talk)
- Maintain team connection — attend sessions as a supporter/observer

### Part 3 — Comeback Mindset Session (8 min)

Coach reads a (real or fictional) story of an athlete who used injury time to transform their mental game. Discussion:

- "What mindset shift made the difference?"
- "What would you do with 6 weeks off injured?"
- "How can injury make you a better athlete?"

Players write a personal "If I was injured tomorrow" plan.

### Part 4 — Injury Support Guide (2 min)

```
MY COMEBACK MINDSET

During injury I will:
- Visualise: ____________ (time/day)
- Read/study: ____________
- Continue: ____________ (what mental skills I'll keep practising)
- Connect with: ____________ (team, support network)

My comeback intention: ____________
```

### Safeguarding Note

Injury can trigger significant psychological distress, particularly in athletes with high athletic identity (see Week 28). Monitor closely. Refer to sports psychologist for extended response beyond normal adjustment.

## Week 34: Relationships in Sport

**Module:** Social support | **Core Skill:** Building a support network | **Age Bands:** All | **Duration:** 20 min | **Format:** Trust exercise + support mapping

**Evidence Basis:** Rees & Hardy (2000): perceived social support is one of the strongest predictors of stress resilience in youth athletes. Sarason et al. (1990): the perception of available support matters as much as actual support received.

### Part 1 — Opening (2 min)

> "Nobody becomes their best alone. The best athletes have great support networks. Today we build yours — and strengthen the team's."

### Part 2 — Support Network Types (5 min)

Four categories of support:

1. Emotional: Who listens? Who believes in you?
2. Informational: Who gives you good advice? Who tells you the truth?
3. Practical: Who helps with logistics, injuries, training?
4. Esteem: Who makes you feel capable and valued?

> "Gaps in your support network create vulnerability. A strong team is also a strong support network."

### Part 3 — Trust-Building Exercise (11 min)

- Pair exercise: partner A shares one genuine challenge they're facing in their football (3 min) — no fixing, just listening
- Partner B reflects back: "What I heard you say is..." (2 min)
- Role reversal (5 min total)
- Whole group: "What did it feel like to be genuinely listened to?"

### Part 4 — Support Network Map (2 min)

```
MY SUPPORT NETWORK MAP

Emotional support: ____________ (name + how they help)
Informational: ____________
Practical: ____________
Esteem: ____________
Gap I need to address: ____________
```

### Safeguarding Note

Some children may reveal that they have very limited or no support network. This is a pastoral and potentially safeguarding concern. Follow up privately with pastoral team.

## Week 35: Playing with Freedom

**Module:** Optimal performance | **Core Skill:** Flow state | **Age Bands:** All | **Duration:** 20 min | **Format:** Flow state practice

**Evidence Basis:** Csikszentmihalyi (1990): flow state — challenge-skill balance, clear goals, immediate feedback, loss of self-consciousness. Jackson & Csikszentmihalyi (1999): applied flow research in sport — flow is achievable with deliberate mental conditions.

### Part 1 — Opening (3 min)

> "Have you ever had a game where everything just worked? Time slowed down. You just knew what to do before you did it. You weren't thinking — just playing."

> "That's called a flow state. It's not accidental. We can set up the conditions for it."

### Part 2 — Flow Conditions (5 min)

Seven conditions for flow in sport:

1. Challenge slightly exceeds skill (not too easy, not too hard)
2. Clear process goals (not outcomes)
3. Unambiguous feedback (you know how you're doing)
4. Deep focus (no distraction)
5. Sense of control
6. Loss of self-consciousness
7. Transformation of time

> "You can't force flow. But you can remove what blocks it: outcome anxiety, self-consciousness, distraction. That's what all your training has been about."

### Part 3 — Freedom Drill (10 min)

- Design a drill at exactly the right challenge level (coach adjusts dynamically)
- Rules: no scorekeeping, no coach feedback during, complete silence from observers
- Intent: express what you know — don't try to be perfect
- Coach watches for flow indicators: rhythm, fluency, expressiveness

### Part 4 — Flow State Journal (2 min)

```
MY FLOW STATE JOURNAL

Describe your last flow moment in sport: ____________
What were the conditions? ____________
What was different about your mindset? ____________
What can I replicate before my next game? ____________
```

### Age Adaptations

- **Juniors (7–9):** Use "your best football face — just having fun" framing. Remove all evaluation. Play for joy.
- **Seniors (13–14):** Deep dive into the psychology. Discuss how to recreate flow conditions after a disruption.

## Week 36: Phase 3 Review

**Module:** Consolidation | **Core Skill:** Application under pressure | **Age Bands:** All | **Duration:** 25 min | **Format:** Competition debrief + full toolkit review

### Part 1 — Competition Debrief (10 min)

Review a recent real competitive performance (match from the previous week):

- Each player uses the Week 27 post-game review format
- Focus: "Did you use any mental fitness tools in the game? Did they work?"

### Part 2 — Full Toolkit Inventory (10 min)

Players work through their complete toolkit (Phases 1, 2, and 3 combined):

- Which tools are now automatic?
- Which tools do they still have to consciously deploy?
- What's their single biggest mental fitness development in the last 9 months?

### Part 3 — Phase 3 Certificate and Intent (5 min)

> "You've done three quarters of a year of mental fitness training. You're now equipped with more psychological tools than most adult athletes. Phase 4 is about making it yours. Permanently."

```
FULL TOOLKIT REVIEW — PHASE 3

My most automatic mental skill: ____________
My biggest mental fitness transformation this year: ____________
What I want to master in Phase 4: ____________
```
