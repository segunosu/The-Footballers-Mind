## Week 13: Self-Talk That Works

**Module:** Positive self-instruction | **Core Skill:** Internal dialogue management | **Age Bands:** All | **Duration:** 15 min | **Format:** Awareness + cue word practice

**Evidence Basis:** Hatzigeorgiadis et al. (2011) meta-analysis (k=32): self-talk interventions significantly improve sport performance, especially for tasks requiring precision. Hardy et al. (2009): instructional self-talk aids technique acquisition; motivational self-talk aids effort and endurance.

### Part 1 — Opening (2 min)

> "What do you say to yourself after a bad touch? If your best friend spoke to you the way you speak to yourself after a mistake — would they still be your friend?"

(Uncomfortable laughter. Let it land.)

> "The voice in your head is the most powerful coach you'll ever have. Today we hire a better one."

### Part 2 — The Three Self-Talk Types (4 min)

1. Instructional: "Keep your head still. Weight the pass."
2. Motivational: "I can do this. I've done this before."
3. Disruptive (automatic): "You idiot. Don't mess up. They're all watching."

> "We can't delete Type 3 — but we can replace it. Not with fake positivity. With functional statements."

**Cue word practice:**

> "A cue word is short, personal, and activating. Not 'I am the greatest.' Something like: Sharp. Ready. Trust. Fight. Mine. Yours is different from everyone else's."

Each player selects and tests their primary cue word.

### Part 3 — Cue Word in Drill (7 min)

- Shooting or set-piece drill
- Before every attempt: player must say cue word (out loud or mouthed)
- After bad execution: say cue word + instructional self-talk (not disruptive)
- Coach reinforces process over outcome

### Part 4 — Close + Takeaway (2 min)

> "Your cue word is free. It goes everywhere with you. Use it before every difficult moment this week."

**Self-talk script template:**

```
MY SELF-TALK SCRIPT
My cue word: ____________
Before a set piece I say: ____________
After a mistake I say: ____________
When I'm low on confidence I say: ____________
```

### Age Adaptations

- **Juniors (7–9):** One word only. Make it fun — superhero name, animal, colour. Draw it on their wristband.
- **Seniors (13–14):** Explore the research on choking. Why does "don't miss" backfire? Teach process focus.

### Safeguarding Note

Internal dialogue work can surface negative self-concept. If a child's spontaneous self-talk reveals significant self-loathing or hopelessness, flag for pastoral support.

## Week 14: Imagery and Visualisation

**Module:** Mental rehearsal | **Core Skill:** Imagery | **Age Bands:** All | **Duration:** 15 min | **Format:** Guided visualisation + on-pitch application

**Evidence Basis:** Cumming & Williams (2012): imagery is one of the most widely used psychological skills across sport; vividness and controllability are key predictors of effectiveness. Ranganathan et al. (2004): mental practice activates the same motor neurons as physical practice.

### Part 1 — Opening (2 min)

> "Before I explain this — close your eyes for 10 seconds. Picture yourself scoring the perfect goal. Where are you? What does it feel like? What do you hear?"

(Pause.)

> "What you just did fired the same neurons in your brain as if you'd actually done it. That's not magic. That's neuroscience."

### Part 2 — How to Visualise (4 min)

Four keys to effective imagery:

1. Vividness — see it in colour, with sound, with feeling
2. Controllability — you control the outcome, not watch it happen
3. Internal perspective — see it through your own eyes, not from outside
4. Positive execution — always rehearse success (not "don't miss")

**Guided 90-second imagery script:**

> "Close your eyes. You're about to take a free kick. Feel your feet on the grass. The weight of the ball. You take three steps back. Breathe. Your cue word. You see where it's going. Strike it perfectly. Watch it go in. How do you feel?"

### Part 3 — Pre-Game Visualisation Drill (7 min)

- Before a small-sided game, everyone does 60 seconds of individual visualisation (eyes closed, silent)
- Players visualise their best performance in the specific game about to happen
- Then play — debrief how it felt to step in with a rehearsed mindset

### Part 4 — Close + Takeaway (2 min)

> "Your brain trains 24 hours a day. Every time you visualise successfully, you're training. No pitch needed."

**Session replay journal:**

```
SESSION REPLAY JOURNAL
After every session this week, write:
- One moment I performed well → replay it in detail 3x tonight
- One moment I want to improve → visualise the corrected version 3x
```

### Age Adaptations

- **Juniors (7–9):** Use "superhero movie" framing. "Close your eyes — you're in your highlight reel." Keep it under 30 seconds.
- **Seniors (13–14):** Add emotional imagery (feel the pressure, then the regulation). Introduce "PETTLEP" model (Holmes & Collins, 2001).

### Safeguarding Note

Some children with trauma histories may find closed-eye visualisation distressing. Offer an open-eye alternative. Never force participation.

## Week 15: Dealing with Criticism

**Module:** Feedback processing | **Core Skill:** Constructive response to criticism | **Age Bands:** All | **Duration:** 15 min | **Format:** Discussion + role play + drill

**Evidence Basis:** Dweck (2006): children with growth mindsets treat criticism as information, not verdict. Horn (2002): coach feedback is the single biggest environmental factor in youth athlete self-perceptions.

### Part 1 — Opening (2 min)

> "When a coach gives you feedback mid-game, what happens in your body? Do you switch on or switch off?"

> "Today we train one of the most underrated skills in sport: hearing criticism and getting better because of it — not worse."

### Part 2 — Three Types of Feedback Response (4 min)

- Reactive: Defensive, dismissive, upset. Performance drops.
- Compliant: Accept it on the surface, ruminate later. Performance fluctuates.
- Curious: "What does this tell me? What can I adjust?" Performance improves.

**The feedback filter:**

1. Is this accurate? (Is it true?)
2. Is this useful? (Can I act on it?)
3. Is this mine to fix right now? (Or is it a systemic/tactical issue?)

### Part 3 — Coach Feedback Drill (7 min)

- Technical drill (passing, position play)
- Coach gives specific (sometimes challenging) feedback during the drill: "That was too slow." "Your positioning is wrong." "Do it again."
- Players practise the Curious response: acknowledge, repeat the correction aloud, adjust, execute
- Rule: zero visible frustration responses. Not suppression — redirection.

### Part 4 — Reflection Protocol (2 min)

> "The best feedback you'll ever get is from a coach who cares enough to tell you the truth. Teach yourself to receive it."

```
FEEDBACK REFLECTION PROTOCOL
Feedback I received this week: ____________
Was it accurate? ____________
Was it useful? ____________
My adjustment: ____________
```

### Age Adaptations

- **Juniors (7–9):** Coach frames criticism as "help" explicitly. Use warm tone. Focus on "try it this way" language.
- **Seniors (13–14):** Discuss how to respond to public criticism (scouts, parents, social media). Introduce emotional differentiation between "feedback about my performance" and "feedback about me as a person."

### Safeguarding Note

Some criticism in sport is not constructive — it is abusive. Teach children the difference. If a child reports coach behaviour that sounds belittling, humiliating, or targeting, this must be treated as a safeguarding concern.

## Week 17: Confidence Rituals

**Module:** Pre-performance activation | **Core Skill:** Ritual-based confidence | **Age Bands:** All | **Duration:** 15 min | **Format:** Ritual design + test

**Evidence Basis:** Damisch et al. (2010) — superstitious rituals improve performance by increasing self-efficacy and perceived control. Cotterill (2010): the function, not the content, of a pre-performance routine is what matters.

### Part 1 — Opening (2 min)

> "What do professional athletes do before a match — the specific rituals? Some are public. Some are private. Why?"

> "Rituals aren't superstition. They're a signal to your nervous system: I'm ready. I've done this before. It's time."

### Part 2 — Ritual Design (5 min)

Three ritual components:

1. Physical (touch a specific part of kit, specific warm-up action)
2. Mental (a phrase, a memory, a visualisation)
3. Communal (a handshake, a team gesture)

> "Your ritual should take 30–60 seconds. It should be repeatable in any environment — home pitch, away ground, cup final."

Players design their ritual with a partner. Coach challenges: "What if you forgot your lucky socks? Your ritual should work without props."

### Part 3 — Ritual Design and Test (6 min)

- Players perform ritual before a pressure drill (e.g., penalty shoot-out format)
- After each round: "Did your ritual help you arrive differently?"
- Video review option: watch yourself performing your ritual (phones or coach's device)

### Part 4 — Close + Takeaway (2 min)

> "Your ritual is now part of your game. Write it down. Practise it until you don't have to think about it."

```
MY CONFIDENCE RITUAL
Physical action: ____________
Mental phrase/image: ____________
Communal gesture: ____________
Total time: _____ seconds
```

### Age Adaptations

- **Juniors (7–9):** Keep ritual to one simple physical action (e.g., touch your wristband, say your word). Pair with a partner ritual for fun.
- **Seniors (13–14):** Discuss the psychology of rituals in high-stakes contexts (surgeons, pilots, athletes). Explore the neuroscience of perceived control.

## Week 18: The Confidence Bank

**Module:** Mastery tracking | **Core Skill:** Evidence-based confidence | **Age Bands:** All | **Duration:** 15 min | **Format:** Reflection + drill + journaling

**Evidence Basis:** Bandura (1997): mastery experiences are the #1 source of self-efficacy. Keeping explicit records of achievement accelerates confidence development (Zimmerman, 2000, self-regulation research).

### Part 1 — Opening (2 min)

> "Confidence without evidence is wishful thinking. Confidence WITH evidence is unshakeable. Today we start building your evidence base."

### Part 2 — The Confidence Bank (4 min)

Explain the metaphor:

> "Every time you do something well — a good tackle, a controlled first touch, staying calm under pressure, recovering from a mistake — that goes into your confidence bank. Your brain can access those deposits when it needs them."

> "Most players forget their deposits. They remember every withdrawal (mistake) and forget every deposit. We're flipping that."

Introduce the Wins Journal — players write 3 deposits from the last 7 days.

### Part 3 — Confidence Deposit Drill (7 min)

- Technical mastery drill — designed to guarantee success (lower difficulty than normal)
- Every successful repetition: player acknowledges it ("bank it" gesture — fist to chest)
- Coach reinforces specific deposits: "That's in your bank. That's evidence."
- End with higher-difficulty version — draw on the deposits

### Part 4 — Close + Takeaway (2 min)

> "Your confidence bank never goes to zero. You just need to remember what's in it."

```
WINS JOURNAL
Date | What I did well | What it tells me
_____ | _______________ | _______________
```

(Players commit to 3 entries per week for the next 4 weeks.)

### Age Adaptations

- **Juniors (7–9):** Use stickers or stamps in a physical journal. Visual and tangible.
- **Seniors (13–14):** Track over a month. Look for patterns — when is confidence high? What depletes it? Data-driven self-coaching.

## Week 19: Handling Mistakes in the Moment

**Module:** In-game recovery | **Core Skill:** Rapid reset | **Age Bands:** All | **Duration:** 15 min | **Format:** Reset protocol + drill

**Evidence Basis:** Beilock & Carr (2001): dwelling on mistakes mid-performance causes choking by overloading working memory. Loehr & Schwartz (2003): the 16-second recovery ritual (used by top tennis players) reduces performance disruption.

### Part 1 — Opening (2 min)

> "Most players lose two moments when they make a mistake. The moment the mistake happened — and the NEXT moment, because they're still thinking about it."

> "Today we train the reset. Five seconds between the mistake and the next moment."

### Part 2 — The Quick Reset Protocol (4 min)

Three-step in-game reset:

1. Release (physical: shake, clap, exhale sharply)
2. Reset (cue word + one breath)
3. Refocus (look up, pick a target, move)

Total time: under 5 seconds.

> "You don't process the mistake in the game. You process it after. During the game — release, reset, refocus. That's your job."

Practise the sequence 5 times as a group.

### Part 3 — Quick Reset Drill (7 min)

- Technical drill with deliberate errors built in (uneven ball, coach interference)
- After EVERY error: player executes the 3-step reset sequence before their next touch
- Coach enforces it — no next touch until reset is performed
- Second round: untimed, players self-regulate

### Part 4 — Close + Takeaway (2 min)

> "Five seconds. Three steps. That's all that separates a mistake from a recovery."

```
MY MISTAKE RESET CARD
1. Release: ____________ (my physical release action)
2. Reset: ____________ (my cue word + breath)
3. Refocus: ____________ (where I look / what I pick as my target)
```

### Age Adaptations

- **Juniors (7–9):** Use "shake it off" — literally shake the whole body. One-step reset only.
- **Seniors (13–14):** Introduce the research on working memory and choking. Discuss why the reset prevents the spiral.

## Week 20: Coach-Led Session (Adult Integration)

**Module:** Adult integration | **Core Skill:** Parent and coach engagement | **Age Bands:** All | **Duration:** 30 min | **Format:** Joint parent/coach + player session

**Evidence Basis:** Fraser-Thomas et al. (2005): parental climate is the #1 predictor of youth athlete mental wellbeing. Harwood & Knight (2009) — educating parents about psychological skills reduces negative sideline behaviour and improves athlete experience.

### Part 1 — Parent/Coach Opening (5 min)

[Delivered to adults AND players together]

> "Welcome. Today is different. This session is for everyone. Because the research is clear: the most powerful mental fitness influence on a young athlete is the adult in their environment — parent and coach."

> "What you say in the car home after a match. What you shout from the sideline. Whether you focus on effort or outcome. All of it shapes their mental game."

### Part 2 — Adult Education (10 min)

Three evidence-based principles for adults:

1. Process over outcome: "How did you feel?" not "Did you win?"
2. Mistake framing: "What did you learn?" not "Why did you do that?"
3. Autonomy support: "What do you think you should do?" not constant instruction

Group discussion: adults share what they currently say/do. Coach facilitates honest reflection.

### Part 3 — Joint Drill (12 min)

Paired parent/player or coach/player drill:

- Technical drill where adult gives only positive specific feedback
- Then: adult gives a growth mindset response to an error (scripted)
- Debrief: "How did that feel different?"

### Part 4 — Family Conversation Guide (3 min)

> "Tonight's homework is for ADULTS, not players."

```
FAMILY CONVERSATION GUIDE
After the next match:
1. "How did you feel during the game?" (Wait. Listen fully.)
2. "What was the hardest moment mentally?" (No fixing — just listen.)
3. "What are you most proud of — regardless of the result?" (Effort/character focus.)
Skip: "Why did you miss that?" / "You should have…" / "That was your fault."
```

### Safeguarding Note

This session may surface difficult parent-child dynamics. Coach facilitates, does not counsel. If a parent's relationship with a child's sport performance appears harmful, follow club safeguarding protocols.

## Week 21: Focus Under Fatigue

**Module:** Concentration | **Core Skill:** Late-game focus | **Age Bands:** All | **Duration:** 20 min | **Format:** Fatigue drill + focus protocol

**Evidence Basis:** McMorris & Graydon (2000): physical fatigue significantly impairs executive function (decision-making, attention) without proper management. Pageaux et al. (2015): mental fatigue directly impairs physical performance — the relationship is bidirectional.

### Part 1 — Opening (2 min)

> "How is your decision-making in the 80th minute compared to the 10th? Honest answers."

> "When your body is tired, your brain is the first thing to go. That's why the best teams score late — and the worst ones concede."

### Part 2 — The Fatigue-Focus Protocol (4 min)

Four strategies for maintaining focus when physically tired:

1. Simplify your cue: one-word focus (not a full sentence)
2. Reduce your decision window: look for ONE thing, not everything
3. Breathe to reset: even 5 seconds of diaphragmatic breathing restores attention
4. Anchor to the present: "Next action. Not the game. This moment."

### Part 3 — Late-Game Focus Drill (12 min)

- Hard physical conditioning block (7 min — coach's choice of intensity)
- Immediately after: demanding small-sided game (5 min) with no recovery
- Mandate: players must verbalise one focus cue before every touch
- Coach adds pressure: "You're down 1-0. Two minutes left. This is your season."
- Debrief: "What happened to your focus? What pulled you back in?"

### Part 4 — Close + Takeaway (2 min)

> "Mental fitness isn't just for when you're fresh. It's exactly when your body is failing that your mind needs to lead."

```
FATIGUE + FOCUS LOG
After your next hard training session, record:
At full energy, my focus was: ___/10
At end of session, my focus was: ___/10
What pulled me back: ____________
```

### Age Adaptations

- **Juniors (7–9):** Reduce physical intensity. Focus on "what's one thing you need to do RIGHT NOW?" — present-moment anchor.
- **Seniors (13–14):** Introduce dual-task performance research. Discuss how elite players maintain technical quality under fatigue.

## Week 22: Goal Setting

**Module:** Motivation | **Core Skill:** Goal-setting | **Age Bands:** All | **Duration:** 20 min | **Format:** Goal-setting workshop + drill

**Evidence Basis:** Locke & Latham (2002): goal-setting is one of the most replicated performance interventions in psychology. Burton et al. (2001, sport meta-analysis): process + performance goals outperform outcome goals for youth athletes.

### Part 1 — Opening (2 min)

> "What do you want to achieve in football this season? Give me an honest answer — not what you think I want to hear."

(Responses: play more, score more, get into academy, improve technically.)

> "Goals are powerful. But only certain types of goals drive performance. Today we build the right kind."

### Part 2 — Three Types of Goals (5 min)

1. Outcome goals: Win the league, get scouted (motivating, but outside your control)
2. Performance goals: Score 5 goals this season, reduce errors by 20% (partially in your control)
3. Process goals: Hit 3 specific targets every training session (fully in your control)

> "The research is clear: process goals are the most powerful driver of improvement. They're also the most ignored."

SMART goals recap: Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound.

### Part 3 — SMART Goal Activity (10 min)

Each player constructs one goal in each category:

- One outcome goal (dream)
- One performance goal (season target)
- Two process goals (this week's targets)

Partner check: is each goal SMART? Can it be measured? Is the process goal achievable this week?

### Part 4 — Goal Card (3 min)

```
MY GOAL CARD
Dream outcome: ____________
Season performance target: ____________
This week's process goals:
1. ____________
2. ____________
Review date: ____________
```

### Age Adaptations

- **Juniors (7–9):** Two goals only — "one big goal" and "one thing I'll try every practice."
- **Seniors (13–14):** Add long-term goals (12 months, 3 years). Discuss gap between dream and current reality — and the process that bridges it.

## Week 23: Playing Through Nerves

**Module:** Anxiety reframe | **Core Skill:** Pre-competition anxiety management | **Age Bands:** All | **Duration:** 20 min | **Format:** Pre-competition simulation

**Evidence Basis:** Martens et al. (1990): competitive state anxiety has cognitive and somatic components; cognitive reappraisal is more effective than suppression. Hanton & Jones (1999): elite athletes interpret pre-competition anxiety as facilitative; non-elite athletes interpret it as debilitative.

### Part 1 — Opening (3 min)

> "Who gets nervous before a big game? (Hands up.) Good. That means you care. Anyone who says they never get nervous is either lying or not competing at a level that challenges them."

> "Nerves are not the enemy. The enemy is what you do with them."

### Part 2 — The Nerves-as-Energy Script (5 min)

Four reframe statements (coach reads aloud, players repeat):

- "My nerves mean this matters to me."
- "My body is getting ready to perform."
- "I've been here before. I know what to do."
- "I am excited."

Introduce the pre-competition activation sequence:

1. Breathing (physiological sigh ×2)
2. Cue word
3. One confidence memory (10 seconds)
4. Intent word for today's performance

### Part 3 — Pre-Competition Routine Simulation (10 min)

- Set up a high-stakes scenario (tournament format, points on a board, spectators invited)
- Before starting: everyone goes through their full pre-competition routine (5 minutes)
- Play — coach notes who shows signs of debilitative anxiety vs. facilitative
- Debrief: "What was different about doing a full routine before this compared to normally?"

### Part 4 — Close + Takeaway (2 min)

> "Elite athletes don't eliminate nerves. They use them. You just started practising that."

```
NERVES AS ENERGY SCRIPT
Before my next competitive situation:
1. I breathe: ____________
2. I say: ____________
3. I remember: ____________
4. My word today: ____________
```

### Age Adaptations

- **Juniors (7–9):** "Butterflies in your tummy? They're helping you fly." Simple physical cue + fun reframe.
- **Seniors (13–14):** Introduce somatic anxiety measurement (CSAI-2). Have them track their pre-competition anxiety over 3 games and look for patterns.

### Safeguarding Note

If a child presents with severe pre-competition anxiety (vomiting, refusal to play, panic attacks), this is beyond sport psychology scope and requires professional support.

## Week 24: Phase 2 Review

**Module:** Tools audit | **Core Skill:** Integration and consolidation | **Age Bands:** All | **Duration:** 25 min | **Format:** Toolbox presentation + self-review

### Part 1 — Opening (2 min)

> "Phase 2 is done. You have 12 more tools. Today you take inventory."

### Part 2 — Toolbox Presentation (10 min)

In groups of 3, players present their "mental fitness toolbox":

- What tools do they have?
- Which ones are most automatic?
- Which ones fall apart under pressure?
- What's the gap?

Each group shares one insight with the full group.

### Part 3 — Skills Stress Test (10 min)

High-pressure small-sided game with active stressors:

- Score announced mid-game (to create pressure)
- Coach makes a controversial decision
- One player is "watched" by a guest observer

Players must use tools proactively — no prompting.

### Part 4 — Self-Review Card (3 min)

```
PHASE 2 SELF-REVIEW
Tool | Automatic under pressure? | Needs more practice?
Self-talk | ☐ Yes / ☐ Not yet | ☐
Imagery | ☐ Yes / ☐ Not yet | ☐
Handling criticism | ☐ Yes / ☐ Not yet | ☐
Composure | ☐ Yes / ☐ Not yet | ☐
Confidence rituals | ☐ Yes / ☐ Not yet | ☐
Confidence bank | ☐ Yes / ☐ Not yet | ☐
Mistake reset | ☐ Yes / ☐ Not yet | ☐
Focus under fatigue | ☐ Yes / ☐ Not yet | ☐
Goal setting | ☐ Yes / ☐ Not yet | ☐
Nerves reframe | ☐ Yes / ☐ Not yet | ☐
```

### Age Adaptations

- **Juniors (7–9):** Simple sticker chart — which tools have I tried? Which am I proud of?
- **Seniors (13–14):** Written performance analysis: "Which tool has changed my game the most?"
